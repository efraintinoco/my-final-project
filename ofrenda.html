"Ofrenda"

Day of the Dead is a tradition that goes back thousands of years. It goes all the way back to the Mesoamerican civilizations of Central and Southern Mexico. The centerpiece of Day of the Dead is known as an "ofrenda". An ofrenda, which may be quite large and elaborate, is usually created by the family members of a person who has died and is intended to welcome the deceased family member to the home. I take great joy in putting together an ofrenda every year. Ofrendas are not only a great way to honor the dead, but putting one together is also a great bonding experience for the entire family ! Offerings for the Ofrenda


  • Table with multiple tiers
  • Candles
  • Food and drinks
  • Photographs
  • Marigold flowers
  • Sugar Skulls

The Bottom Tier is the most practical. This is filled with candles, to light the way, and also water so the returning souls can refresh themselves from the long journey. This tier, at times, may also include a basin and razor for washing and grooming upon arrival. The candles may also be decorated with pictures and embellisments.This is where you also find the Marigold flowers, as they are said to help lead the departed souls home.

The Second Tier is used to place items such as food, drinks, and personal belongings for the returning souls. This tier includes traditional foods such as tamales, sugarcane, fruit, and pan de muertos. For ofrendas calling on deceased adults, servings of tequila or beer are common. For children, their favorite toy is usually included in the display.

The Top Tier is used to identify the person (or persons) for whom the ofrenda was created. Here you will find several photographs of when they were alive, sitting on the top tier along with various religious items. This is also where you will find the sugar skulls. Sugar skulls, which represent the departed souls, also reflect the traditional folk art style, with big happy smiles and colorful icing decorations. Making the sugar skulls are a great family bonding experience. Try it for yourself !
